A visual novel that teaches advanced English vocabulary through a murder mystery set in 1920s Egypt — featuring Detective Poirot, branching investigation paths, and three original minigames.

Concept

This game is designed for advanced English learners preparing for the GRE and TOEFL. Unlike typical vocabulary apps, it teaches abstract, multi-meaning words through a rich narrative context — a detective story where every clue, conversation, and deduction reinforces the target vocabulary.

Players follow Detective Poirot as he investigates a series of mysterious deaths connected to an Egyptian pharaoh's tomb. The twist: Poirot, a Belgian, occasionally stumbles over English — creating natural, humorous moments where vocabulary is introduced, explained, and reinforced through character dialogue.

"The game incorporates learning into an engaging, meaningful story and makes each part replayable — combining input and output stages while offering the learner agency to explore the game world."

Storyline

The Adventure of the Egyptian Tomb

The story unfolds across four chapters, each deepening the mystery and expanding the player's vocabulary. The tone is mysterious yet humorous, highlighted by continuous banter between Poirot and his assistant Hastings.

Ch 1 · The First Death

Two archaeological teams witness the opening of Pharaoh Men-Her-Ra's tomb. Sir Willard, who dismissed the curse, dies after entering.

Ch 2 · The Second Death

Lady Willard hires Poirot. The excavation sponsor dies of blood poisoning. Hastings investigates in New York — and discovers a body.

Ch 3 · The Trip to Egypt

Poirot and Hastings travel to Egypt. The player chooses who to interrogate — each suspect reveals different pieces of the puzzle.

Ch 4 · Final Battle

The murderer attempts to kill Poirot. The player must assemble all collected clues into a chain of evidence to reach the good ending.

Chapter 3 — Investigation Branches:

The player can interrogate four suspects in any order, each unlocking different information:

Sir Guy Willard

The young nobleman continuing his father's work — a firm believer in science who despises the notion of supernatural power.

Dr. Ames

The team doctor. Claims all deaths were natural causes. Poirot challenges him: the power of superstition can also be powerful.

Harper, the Secretary

Knows many things about the Bleibners. A complex relationship — with a story that must be unlocked by the player.

Hassan, the Servant

Firmly believes in the curse. Asks Poirot to protect the young Sir — adding tension between science and superstition.

Unlockable secrets: the Bleibner family relationships, Rupert Bleibner's past and personality, the existence of a will, local tales connected to the tomb.

After all investigations, Poirot reveals that the deaths mirror ancient tales from a "magic book" — and then a more horrible spectacle takes place...

Learning Design

How the Game Teaches

The game targets both meaning and usage of advanced vocabulary. Meaning includes spelling, definitions, synonyms/antonyms, and etymology. Usage involves deduction, induction, and analysis — verbal logic skills tested on the GRE.

Vocabulary in dialogue — natural input:

Poirot: "I am the best detective in the world!"
Hastings: "My friend, you are overselling yourself again!"
Poirot: "Selling what? I don't get it."
Hastings: "It's the combination of 'over' and 'sell', only in a figurative way!"

Words and glosses are embedded in dialogue with no extra clicks — the flow of conversation stays intact. Three original minigames reinforce the vocabulary through different cognitive channels:

Minigames

Three Ways to Practice

Minigame 1 · Etymology Builder

Drag-and-drop word roots and prefixes to assemble vocabulary. Confusing pairs (de/di, hyper/hypo, pre/per/pro) are added as distractors. Hover for glosses; quotes from the scene aid recall.

Minigame 2 · Timed Card Match

Flip cards to match words with synonyms or definitions under time pressure. Pictorial glosses on definition cards trigger recall. No win/lose — but faster completion earns more rewards.

Minigame 3 · Sentence Equivalence

Fill blanks with synonyms (or antonyms) to make sentences logically correct. Correct answers unlock hidden story secrets — connecting vocabulary practice to narrative reward.

Design Framework

Theoretical Foundations

The game's learning design is grounded in established SLA and cognitive science frameworks, adapted for a game-based context.

Task-Based Learning

Authentic tasks (spotting clues, building evidence) drive vocabulary acquisition through meaningful use.

Contextualized Learning

Glosses embedded in dialogue, visualized in scenes, and tied to emotional/narrative moments.

Dual Coding

Pictorial + textual glosses work together to strengthen encoding without increasing cognitive load.

AGES Model

Attention (timed tasks), Generation (player autonomy), Emotion (surprises), Spacing (plot/minigame alternation).

SLA Theory Implementation:

FrameworkApproaches Used
StructuralDrill & practice; corrective feedback across game modes; translation of both meaning and usage
CognitiveMultiple output modes; working memory tasks under time pressure; gap negotiation; strategy-dependent gameplay; experiential learning

AGES Model in Detail:

ComponentHow It's Promoted
AttentionCountdown choices, color-tagged text, timed minigames, final deduction under pressure
GenerationPlayer chooses investigation branches and reward strategies; connects known roots to new words
EmotionSurprise reveals, humorous character dialogue, narrative tension and rewards
SpacingAlternation between main plot and minigames; sparse information delivery to avoid cognitive overload

The game also applies the Involvement Load Hypothesis: the detective story creates intrinsic motivation through curiosity and suspense, while the minigame reward system provides extrinsic motivation for replay. Input Enhancement is achieved through color coding, audio effects, and extensive repetition across contexts.

Design Decisions

Key Considerations

Instructions

Kept deliberately short — bite-sized and discoverable via Help buttons. The game is replayable, so players learn by doing.

Language Register

Balanced: some characters (a Belgian detective, a servant) speak simply; others (English nobles, scholars) model advanced usage naturally within the fiction.

Feedback Design

Varies by stage — explicit corrective feedback early on, implicit contextual feedback later. Metalinguistic feedback from characters. Difficulty increases gradually.

Difficulty Staircase

Vocabulary scope and task complexity increase as the player progresses. Rest points between chapters allow consolidation. The stairs are designed to avoid both boredom and frustration.
